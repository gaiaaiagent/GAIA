## Gregory's Background & The Genesis of Regen Network

Gregory's path to founding Regen Network came through frustration with existing systems. His livelihood before 2017 was "permaculture design and consulting," though "the last three years of that arc, I had been working on supply chain work with brands, so trying to do broadacre, regenerative agriculture, and kind of like economic transformation work with companies."

This experience proved "pretty frustrating because the system, the larger economic system and social system and legal system are all really not built for regenerative reciprocity. The incentives aren't aligned. People, you know, if a company has an issue, they have a cash crunch or they have an emergency of some sort, you know, you always end up getting these chains of defection out of the regenerative sort of process that's underway."

The pattern was predictable and disheartening: "It's the ecosystem and the farmers, essentially, the land stewards that end up suffering. And it's just statistically almost certain that that's how it's going to go. That's just the way the field is just tipped in that direction."

In 2017, having been "a crypto investor since 2015" and "paying attention to things and interested, but skeptical," Gregory and collaborators decided to "go all in and see if we couldn't start something that would use that Overton window shift of what was taking place in crypto around what is money, what is value? Is it a dog coin? Is it digital gold? Is it the internet computer? What is it?"

Their approach was to "take a crack at saying what we believe, which is that all value is derived from ecological health and the health of the biosphere and the relationship between humans and the biosphere as stewards and custodians and the keystone species. And that's the crux of things right there."

## Technical Implementation Challenges

The technical journey wasn't straightforward. "When we launched, we weren't really willing to put smart contracts on Ethereum, which at that point was proof of work and had no clear roadmap of shifting to proof of stake. So we were pretty emphatically choosing not to be in the Ethereum world at that point. And the only real viable option at that point was Tendermint consensus and the Cosmos tech stack."

This led to a challenging development process: "We just basically started eating glass and crashing through the monstrosity of spinning up our own blockchain to do the thing that we wanted to do." The technical implementation uses "Golang. It's Go. There's some stuff in Rust, too."

## Current State & Strategic Crossroads

Regen Network today operates with "a registry system that allows people to issue ecological assets according to a standard program. So we have a lot of really high quality ecological assets on chain that companies buy and project developers issue and there's, you know, non trivial activity there, you know, in the millions, you know, sub $10 million a year, but in the millions of dollars a year of transaction volume of people originating and buying and selling ecological assets."

While "that's growing, it's a growth area," Gregory acknowledged it's "not nearly enough in terms of transaction volume and trading and other applications to get the type of transaction volume" needed. More importantly, "it's not very legible to crypto investors in terms of like, or DeFi narratives, it's, you know, so we have a very low market cap chain."

This has led to active exploration: "We are in active discovery process around potential migration to L1s to, you know, like re-deploying as a DAP instead of our own chain, or there's a set of branching patterns that I and other community members are in very active discovery around. The fundamental strategic question facing the network is whether to "just like, be super strictly focused on ecological assets" or "create try to sort of be a shelling point for a broader movement" where "region is a much bigger thing, of which ecological assets are an important part to make legible ecological value. ## AI Agent Architecture Vision

Gregory outlined his experimental approach with AI agents across four levels. "I've been mocking up these three levels of AI agents that I'm starting to experiment with a little bit," though he later clarified it's actually four levels.

**Level 1: Protocol Governance Agents**
"Building governance agents that represent specific tensions and realities and needs at the protocol level of region network." He described programming agents with different orientations: "We might program one to be opinionated that it's really about economic throughput and volume and transactions and users. And we might have another one that's really like, hey, sovereignty and independence and ethics and connection with place."

The vision involves "creating these different archetypes and allowing them to be engaging in a very dynamic way that helps us as humans both see, just like you learned by listening to Ma Earth, we can learn by watching these different positions debate. And also we can start to integrate it into our governance so that there's liquid staking between different positions and coalitions start to form and start to emerge."

**Level 2: Ecological Advocates**
"Eco credit or ecological health advocates that are basically saying, like, I'm a beaver maximalist and I want there to be beavers everywhere. And I'm going to shill the shit out of beaver coin and like bring back the beaver." Or "a soil carbon advocate that's just like regenerative agriculture and soil carbon. It's the most important thing in the world" or "coral reef" advocates.

Colin suggested enhancing this with "a digital twin perspective. So if we could actually have a digital twin of the beavers that has that data available. Hey, it's talking about, OK, there's this many beavers. This is where beavers are endangered. This is areas where beaver habitat is under threat."

**Level 3: Narrative Advocates**
"The narrative advocates for the region, region coin" exploring different economic models. Gregory outlined two primary narratives: "Are we building a regenerative store of value system that's basically like lobbying that, like, you want scarcity, the scarcest thing in the whole universe is life. This is a store of value that's based on life, provable life, it is the best store of value in the universe, right?" 

Alternatively: "Is it a regenerative reserve currency, like this is a currency that's designed to flow and ebb and be careful of rain and all these things. But we don't have to choose one narrative." The key insight: "We have to understand the design dependencies be at the depth of these narratives, they optimize different things. Different things might as well have different impacts on different stakeholders, whether those are beaver stakeholders, or river stakeholders, or stakers of region, etc."

**Level 4: Bioregional Voices**
"The fourth being ecological institutions, but like the voice of a river, the voice of a bioregion, that is actually also owning and staking the network itself."

Gregory's hypothesis: "By creating this hierarchy of agent types, you know, 123, and then the fourth being ecological institutions... we create an incredibly fun, incredibly engaging, incredibly sticky user experience to invite humans to participate with those agents, to help govern them to help guide them to help engage with them. And you know, we can do some really, really cool shit."
