Program Title:
Awaken the Dragon: A Journey of reSTORYing Climate Resilience, Justice, and Wellbeing

Overview:
Participants embark on a mythical journey guided by Drakona, an ancient, intercultural dragon representing wisdom and balance. The program combines climate change education, restorative justice practices, and emotional health support, using storytelling, metaphors, and collaborative activities to foster a deep connection with nature, community, and the self.

Key Themes:
Wisdom of the Dragon: Knowledge of climate science, cultural traditions, and adaptation strategies.
Healing the Earth: Climate justice and reparative actions.
Emotional Resilience: Eco-anxiety and emotional/social well-being.
Unity in Diversity: Intercultural perspectives and collective responsibility.

Book Project Structure:
The journey is divided into five chapters, each representing a "scale" of the dragon. Each chapter builds on the previous, blending education, restorative practices, and storytelling.

Chapter 1: Awakening the Dragon (Understanding the Mythos and Science)
Objective: Build foundational knowledge of climate change and restorative justice.
Activities:
Storytelling: "The Dragon’s Awakening"—a metaphorical tale about the dragon awakening from its slumber to restore balance to a wounded world amd with our awakening on a societal level.
Interactive Lesson: Introduction to local and global climate challenges.
Reflective Circle: “What wisdom would you seek from the dragon?”

Chapter 2: The Dragon’s Flight (Exploring Cultural Perspectives)
Objective: Highlight intercultural approaches to sustainability and justice.
Activities:
Myth Exploration: Students research dragons in different cultures (e.g., Chinese, Celtic, Indigenous traditions) and their symbolic ties to nature and wisdom.
AI Tool: Use platforms like Google Arts & Culture to explore dragon-related myths and climate practices globally.
Restorative Circle: Share how their cultural heritage informs their understanding of justice and environmental responsibility.

Chapter 3: The Dragon’s Fire (Emotional Resilience and Healing)
Objective: Address eco-anxiety and foster emotional health.
Activities:
Metaphorical Exercise: Participants imagine the dragon’s fire as a symbol of inner strength, writing about how they would use it to confront fears and challenges.
AI Mental Health Tools: Tools like Wysa or Woebot guide students through managing eco-anxiety.
Restorative Justice Practice: Role-playing how to repair harm caused by environmental neglect.

Chapter 4: The Dragon’s Scales (Collective Action and Accountability)
Objective: Foster teamwork, accountability, and reparative actions.
Activities:
Group Project: Students collaborate on a climate adaptation or justice project (e.g., planting trees, designing rainwater collection systems).
AI Tools: Use platforms like Zooniverse or Trello for project planning and data collection.
Storytelling: “The Dragon’s Scales” as a metaphor for collective strength and the importance of unity.

Chapter 5: The Dragon’s Roar (Empathy and Advocacy)
Objective: Amplify student voices and advocate for change.
Activities:
Mythopoetic Creation: Students write their own dragon stories, integrating lessons learned and expressing their vision for climate justice.
Advocacy Campaign: Using AI tools like Canva or Animoto, students create digital campaigns to raise awareness in their communities.
Restorative Circle: Reflect on their journey and commit to ongoing action, guided by the metaphor of the dragon’s roar spreading wisdom and change.

Supporting Tools and Technologies:
AI Storytelling Platforms: Use ChatGPT to generate intercultural dragon myths based on student prompts.
Virtual Reality (VR): Incorporate tools like Google Expeditions to immerse students in vulnerable ecosystems (e.g., coral reefs, glaciers).
Data Visualization: Use platforms like Tableau to analyze local climate impacts and project outcomes.

Culminating Event:
The Dragon’s Summit—Students gather to share their stories, present their projects, and celebrate their collective achievements. This event includes:
A storytelling circle, where students narrate their dragon-inspired tales.
A community exhibition of their climate justice projects.
A restorative closing ceremony, where students express gratitude, reflect on their journey, and commit to future actions.

Program Impact:
Cognitive Growth: Deep understanding of climate change and justice principles.
Emotional Resilience: Tools for managing eco-anxiety and fostering mental health.
Social Awareness: Empathy and collaboration through intercultural learning.
Actionable Change: Real-world climate adaptation projects and advocacy.

1. Economic Benefits

The Dragon Walk would serve as a landmark eco-tourism attraction, drawing visitors from across BC, Canada, and internationally.
Benefits:
Increased revenue for local accommodations, restaurants, and artisan businesses.
Sustainable tourism opportunities (e.g., guided Dragon Walks at OUR Ecovillage and new locations in the wider community as well, workshops, and seasonal festivals).
Employment for local guides, cultural educators, and conservation staff.
Economic Multiplier Effect: Tourists typically engage with multiple local businesses, spreading economic benefits throughout the region.
B. Job Creation and Skills Development
Direct Jobs: Roles in planning, constructing, maintaining, and facilitating the Dragon Walk and its learning kiosks.
Education & Training:
Partnerships with local schools, colleges, and Indigenous community members to train folks, particularly youth, in eco-tourism, land stewardship, and storytelling.
Skills development in areas like AR/VR technology, environmental restoration, and community engagement.

Indigenous and local artists could oversee the development of one of the learning kiosks onsite at OUR Ecovillage and ensure that all of the project creates full representation and benefit to the Quw'utsun Peoples and local community.  The Dragon Path would also showcase links to various online & onland venues for any of the Quw'utsun teachings which might be offered by local Knowledge-Keepers and follow Protocol & agreements related to the sharing of local Indigenou arts and culture and that of all wider community artisan crafters.

Sale of art, traditional goods, and locally made products (e.g. carvings, knitting) as culturally appropriate

The Dragon Walk could attract funding and partnerships from:
Provincial and federal grants for Indigenous-led and environmental initiatives.
Corporate sponsorships aligned with sustainability and reconciliation goals.
Investments in green infrastructure and conservation tourism.
A portion of revenues could be reinvested into local climate adaptation projects, benefiting the broader community.

2. Honoring First Peoples and Their Territories
A. Indigenous Leadership and Co-Governance
The project must be Indigenous-led or co-governed to honor the sovereignty and knowledge systems of the First Peoples of the Cowichan Valley and Vancouver Island.
Approaches:
Elders and knowledge-keepers guide the development and implementation of the Dragon Walk.
Establish a co-management structure where decisions about the project, including its profits and impacts, are shared equitably.
Revenue-sharing agreements with Indigenous communities to ensure fair compensation.
B. Revitalization of Indigenous Knowledge
Each kiosk can incorporate teachings, stories, and land-based practices specific to the First Peoples of the region (e.g., Cowichan teachings of respect, responsibility, and reciprocity with the land).
Benefits:
Preserves and shares Indigenous ways of knowing with visitors and future generations.
Creates opportunities for cultural renewal and intergenerational knowledge transfer.
C. Community Economic Empowerment
Indigenous businesses and entrepreneurs could be prioritized for contracts related to:
Construction of the Dragon Walk and kiosks.
Creation of kiosk materials, such as storytelling installations or AR experiences.
Catering, guiding, and event organization for festivals and programs.
D. Connection to the Land
The Dragon Walk would emphasize land-based healing and reciprocity, reflecting the teachings of the First Peoples.
Visitors would:
Contribute to ecological restoration projects (e.g., tree planting, invasive species removal) led by local Indigenous groups.
Develop a deeper understanding of their responsibilities to the land and its caretakers.
E. Alignment with the United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP)
The project could serve as a model for implementing UNDRIP principles by:
Upholding Indigenous self-determination.
Respecting and promoting cultural traditions and knowledge systems.
Ensuring economic activities benefit the First Peoples equitably and sustainably.
